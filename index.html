Connecting Chemistry with Geography

The Periodic Table is a structured chart that organizes all known chemical elements based on their atomic number (number of protons) and recurring chemical properties. The periodic table currently has 118 confirmed elements, ranging from Hydrogen (with 1 atomic number) to Oganesson (with 118 atomic number). At room temperature, about 11 are gases, 2 are liquids (mercury and bromine), and the remaining 105 are solids. Many elements were discovered naturally through observation and mineral studies, while heavier modern elements were created in laboratories using particle accelerators and nuclear reactions.

Many everyday products are made when elements from the periodic table combine into compounds or materials. Think of the periodic table as a toolbox of ingredients. Individual elements often have special properties, but when joined together they can create useful things we use every day. Almost everything around you is made from elements in the periodic table combining with each other.
